Article 1 · Housing Affordability

A City Cannot Be Livable If People Cannot Afford to Live There

By Kassymkhan Juvarov

The City Looks Livable — Until Rent Enters the Picture

A few years ago, I briefly stayed with a friend in Hong Kong. He paid around USD 1,200 a month for a flat barely large enough for daily comfort: a tiny washroom, thin walls, and almost no private space. Outside, Hong Kong looked like a model city — efficient trains, dense employment, strong services, and genuine economic energy. Inside that flat, the word "livable" felt much less convincing.

That contradiction defines the central problem fast-growing cities now face. Housing affordability should not sit beside transport, greenery, and employment as one more livability indicator — it is the condition that decides who can access those urban advantages. If ordinary residents cannot afford decent housing near jobs and services, the city's strengths become visible but unreachable.

When Livability Improvements Price Residents Out

The structural risk for fast-growing cities is not that livability fails to improve — it is that improvement in some domains actively undermines the condition that allows residents to benefit from the rest. Martino et al. (2021) demonstrate this in Metro Vancouver: areas scoring well on accessibility, social diversity, and economic vitality simultaneously saw affordability worsen as land values rose. This is a design failure, not a side effect.

Badland et al. (2014) frame livability through interconnected domains — housing, transport, safety, education, employment, and social cohesion — each linked to health and equity outcomes. Van Kamp et al. (2003) argue that strong objective scores tell planners little unless read alongside residents' lived experience of whether those conditions feel stable and genuinely accessible. Taken together, these frameworks reveal a deeper point: if housing cost forces a resident out, improvements across every other domain produce no benefit for that person. The mutually reinforcing domains Badland et al. describe become irrelevant once affordability gives way. Martino et al.'s (2021) findings confirm this precisely: cities can satisfy objective livability measures while systematically failing the lived condition Van Kamp et al. insist must accompany them.

Affordability as the Gatekeeper

Housing cost shapes the geography of everyday life. When rent outgrows ordinary incomes, residents are pushed into smaller units, overcrowding, insecure tenancies, or distant locations with longer commutes — each weakening access to the same domains Badland et al. (2014) identify as central to urban wellbeing.

Affordability as the Gatekeeper continued

Chan and Wong's (2021) Hong Kong study gives this practical weight: housing expense, living density, housing environment problems, and housing satisfaction each independently predict subjective wellbeing, with private renters bearing the strongest pressure. For planning officers, the implication is precise: housing insecurity is not just a welfare problem outside planning. It is often created by planning decisions about zoning and land supply. Where planning adds density without protecting tenure, it manufactures the very vulnerability Chan and Wong measure.

The Density Trade-Off

Density is often presented as the clean solution: build up, shorten distances, support transit, and reduce land waste. The logic is reasonable — low-density growth carries real costs in transport expenditure, thinner services, and distance from opportunity. But density alone does not guarantee inclusion.

Martino et al. (2021) show the risk clearly. In Metro Vancouver, denser areas performed better on accessibility, diversity, and economic vitality while affordability worsened simultaneously. That is not an argument against density. It is an argument against treating density as automatically progressive. Without affordability protections, density produces a selective form of livability — better urban conditions for those who can pay, and displacement or overcrowding for those who cannot.

“If livability is accessible mainly to high-income residents, it is not livability. It is livability with an entry fee.”

The Affordability Filter

A practical response is to apply an Affordability Filter to every major livability project. Before approving a redevelopment, transit-oriented district, or high-density plan, decision-makers should ask: who can afford to live here after completion, which residents are protected from displacement during the value increase, and does the new supply reduce rent pressure or mainly raise land prices?

The Affordability Filter continued

Vienna shows why this filter must be built into the housing system from the outset, not applied after prices have risen. The city maintains approximately 60 percent of its housing stock in the public or subsidised sector through non-profit partnerships and long-term affordability conditions tied to publicly funded land (Reinprecht, 2014; Housing Europe, 2021). Density increases in inner-city districts have not produced comparable displacement pressures because affordability functions as a design condition rather than an afterthought (Housing Europe, 2021).

Two mechanisms deserve priority. Inclusionary zoning should require a defined share of affordable units — commonly 10 to 25 percent — in major private developments, particularly near new transit or public infrastructure (Calavita & Mallach, 2010). Its effectiveness depends on tight design: affordability benchmarked to local income levels rather than city-wide medians, long-term unit covenants, and restricted in-lieu fee provisions — in high-opportunity locations, the ability to pay a cash substitute rather than build in-place defeats the instrument's purpose entirely. Non-profit and public housing partnerships work through a different mechanism: by holding land in community or public ownership with permanent affordability covenants, they remove stock from speculative resale cycles, structurally guaranteeing affordability rather than leaving it contingent on market conditions.

Livability reporting should complement these mechanisms with rent-burden analysis, displacement-risk mapping, overcrowding rates, and tenure security alongside transport and density targets — making trade-offs visible before a neighbourhood is "improved" for everyone except those priced out of it.

Conclusion

That is why the Hong Kong room matters — and why it matters differently now. At first, it illustrated a contradiction: a livable city containing an unlivable flat. The evidence shows the contradiction is structural. Badland et al.'s (2014) interconnected domains depend on housing as their foundation; Van Kamp et al. (2003) show that objective scores on those domains lose meaning when residents cannot sustain their place in the city; Martino et al. (2021) show that density-led improvement can deepen the affordability losses that Van Kamp et al. identify as erasing its value; and Chan and Wong (2021) close the loop — those losses register most severely among private renters whose vulnerability is a planning output, not a background condition. My friend was not unlucky. He was the predictable outcome of a city that kept improving its livability without asking who could still afford to remain.

For fast-growing cities, the test is direct: who gets to live close enough, securely enough, and affordably enough to benefit from the city being built? If livability is accessible mainly to high-income residents, it is not livability. It is livability with an entry fee.

Article 2 · Urban Sustainability

Green for Whom?

Why fast-growing cities can’t build sustainability without the people who already live there

By Aslbek Makhmudov

An essay on cities, equity, and the people we forget to ask. As metropolitan regions race to meet climate targets, the residents who built the neighbourhoods being “regenerated” are quietly being pushed out. Genuine sustainability has to look different.

The High Line Problem

The High Line is, for me, the cleanest example of the problem. A 1.5-mile elevated park on a disused freight rail in Manhattan, it opened in 2009. Over the next decade, property values around it nearly doubled. Most of the lower-income families who had lived in those streets when planning began were no longer there. That part of the story does not appear in the design magazines.

And it is not a story confined to one Manhattan park.

Atlanta’s BeltLine has produced a similar trajectory. So have parts of the Barcelona superblock programme. An environmental upgrade arrives. Rents follow. The original residents end up living somewhere else. Anguelovski and her co-authors (2019) call this green gentrification. Earlier, Checker (2011) had described the same dynamic as a “greenwave”, and was unsparing about how easily ecological policy becomes a vehicle for class displacement.

For anyone working in urban planning, housing policy, or city development, this should be uncomfortable reading. The profession has gotten remarkably good at counting things. Trees planted. Square metres of permeable pavement. Tons of CO₂ avoided. We have not, by any honest measure, gotten anywhere near as good at counting who gets to stay. In regions where the city is still being drawn, across the Greater Bay Area and much of Southeast Asia, the choices made over the coming decade will set the shape of those neighbourhoods for the rest of the century.

“We are good at counting trees planted. We are not nearly as good at counting who gets to stay.”

Who Gets to Define Sustainability?

My own view is that the trouble starts upstream, with how sustainability gets defined. Open most planning frameworks and you find essentially a technical question: buildings, infrastructure, energy mixes, drainage. The metrics that follow are technical too. Emissions per capita. Energy intensity. Biodiversity scores. Residents tend to appear in these documents as end-users, the people who will eventually consume the infrastructure once it has been built. They are rarely treated as authors of it.

Framing of this kind has consequences. When the people most affected by a plan are not present in the rooms where it is written, the plan drifts in two related directions. It starts to optimise for the wrong outcomes. And the population that ends up benefiting from it turns out, often, to be a noticeably different one from the population it was meant to serve.

Real participation is much harder to do than to claim, which is part of why it gets claimed so often and done so rarely. Sherry Arnstein’s “ladder of citizen participation” (1969) is over fifty years old and still the clearest map of this territory anyone has produced. The bottom rungs are manipulation and tokenism. The hearing held after the real decision has been signed off. The comment box nobody reads. The report whose findings change nothing. The top rungs are partnership, delegated power, and citizen control. Most municipal consultations, if planners are honest about it, sit closer to the bottom of that ladder than to the top.

Participation That Actually Shapes Projects

Some cities have climbed higher up that ladder. Porto Alegre, in Brazil, is the obvious example. For decades, residents there have had direct say over part of the city’s capital budget. Investment in sanitation in poorer districts rose sharply. Medellín, in Colombia, took a different route. Its “library parks” were placed in hillside neighbourhoods the city had long ignored, designed alongside the people who would use them. Safety improved. School enrolment improved. Seyfang and Smith (2007) catalogued dozens of similar experiments. The pattern they describe is consistent. When residents genuinely shape a project, the project tends to fit the place.

Three Shifts That Would Change the Picture

Build anti-displacement measures into greening projects from the start. A new park is, whether anyone says so out loud, a real-estate event. Pairing it with community land trusts, rent-stabilisation zones, or affordable-housing requirements lets current residents still be there when it opens. Atlanta’s BeltLine is a useful warning. The project promised tens of thousands of affordable units, has so far delivered a small fraction, and the resulting displacement was visible in planning documents years in advance. Wolch et al. (2014) call this approach “just green enough”. They argue for greening at a scale that improves environmental quality without setting off speculative pressures that price people out.

Treat equity as a metric, not a value statement. If a project is judged only on trees planted and carbon cut, it can be filed as a success even when half of its original residents have moved away. Equity audits, conducted before approval and repeated after construction, would force planners to ask a more honest set of questions. Who is paying. Who is gaining. Where the gap is widening. Auditing itself is straightforward enough. What is much harder is the political commitment to act on whatever the audit shows, including where it points to a project being redesigned, scaled back, or dropped.

Hand over actual power, not just talking points. Tokenistic consultation has run its course, and a number of cities have started to admit it. What replaces it is less a single mechanism than a layered set of arrangements. Citizen assemblies whose recommendations carry binding weight on neighbourhood-scale decisions. Co-design processes that begin before a master plan is drawn. Community veto rights for projects above a certain size. None of these slow good projects more than litigation and public protest already do.

Conclusion

My broader worry is timing. The amounts being discussed for urban climate adaptation over the coming decade are very large, and a substantial share of that funding will land in regions where the city itself is still being drawn. Whether the result is a generation of cities that are green and broadly fair, or green and sharply unequal, is being settled now, in conversations most residents have no real access to.

A city that meets its climate goals while quietly losing the people who held its working-class districts together is not, by any reading I would call honest, a sustainable city. It is something else. The label has just become useful.

If there is one thing the cities that get this right will end up sharing, it is probably this. Their planners will have stopped treating residents as stakeholders to be managed, and started treating them as people who already know things the planning office does not. Sustainable urbanism that is only lower-carbon is not, in any honest sense, all that sustainable. It has to be lower-carbon and shared.

Article 3 · Public Transport

The Cognitive Tax of the Commute: Redefining Urban Liveability

By Magellan M

This synthesis explores the burgeoning tension between algorithmic urban efficiency and the subjective psychological well-being of the human commuter. By analyzing the “cognitive tax” imposed by modern transit systems, this article challenges the validity of global liveability indices that prioritize throughput over human affect.

The Efficiency Paradox: AI vs. Affect

If a city’s public transit network is a statistically significant predictor of anger, anxiety, and depression, are current global liveability indices relying on fraudulent metrics of success? While Zhao and Chen (2025) emphasize the triumphant narrative of artificial intelligence in maximizing transit efficiency, demonstrating how machine learning can halve service recovery times and drastically reduce road investment costs, Sarmiento-Suarez et al. (2026) counter this mechanical optimism with a sobering psychological reality. They argue that operational efficiency does not inherently produce human well-being, revealing that negative transit experiences function as chronic stressors, yielding a predictive coefficient of β = 0.47 for commuter anger.

Synthesis point: The sheer volumetric throughput championed by algorithmic optimization must be reconciled with the affective, subjective experience of the human commuter. Efficiency without empathy results in a technically functional but socially corrosive infrastructure.

The Digital Panopticon and Spatial Mismatch

This conflict fundamentally destabilizes how we measure urban triumph. The Urban Institute (2024) illustrates how modern mobility-on-demand often exacerbates a “spatial mismatch”, subsidizing frictionless transit for the urban elite while leaving peripheral, transit-dependent populations in “innovation deserts.”

Traditional liveability indices remain willfully blind to these disparities. By prioritizing static infrastructural density over the lived emotional reality of marginalized riders, these indices celebrate a sterile, exclusionary vision of the city.

“A perfectly punctual train filled with anxious, alienated passengers is a profound systemic failure rather than an engineering victory.”

The “Cognitive Tax” Concept

A truly resilient metropolis requires the implementation of a “cognitive tax” deduction, recognizing that a perfectly punctual train filled with anxious, alienated passengers is a profound systemic failure rather than an engineering victory. As we unpack these intersecting paradigms, we must urgently question whether the smart city revolution is engineering a sustainable utopia or merely a hyper-efficient, digital panopticon.

Critical Discussion Questions

  • Metric integration: If we accept the premise of a “cognitive tax,” how might municipal governments quantitatively integrate commuter mental health metrics into their annual transit funding allocations alongside traditional ridership data?
  • Vulnerability and AI: To what extent does the pursuit of AI-driven operational efficiency inherently conflict with the human-centric needs of vulnerable populations, such as those navigating physical or cognitive barriers in the “travel chain”?
  • Structural reform: Do global liveability indices require a fundamental restructuring to account for neighborhood-level transit inequities, or are they irreparably flawed as tools for progressive urban planning?

From Smart Transit to Affective Infrastructure

A stronger version of the argument treats the commute as a multimodal system: not merely a route optimized by algorithms, but a layered social, spatial, sensory, and psychological experience. In this frame, liveability cannot be judged through speed, punctuality, or ridership alone. It must be read through many forms of evidence that show what the journey costs the commuter in attention, comfort, dignity, access, and emotional regulation.

The following evidence stack expands the paper beyond a single efficiency metric and gives planners 15 distinct modalities for detecting the hidden cognitive tax of mobility.

Multimodal Integration Principle

The point is not to create a more invasive smart city, but to resist the false objectivity of a single dashboard. A multimodal model should combine quantitative infrastructure data with qualitative rider experience so that the system can distinguish between a trip that is merely completed and a trip that is humane, legible, accessible, and psychologically sustainable.

Triangulation over surveillance: each modality should be interpreted in relation to the others. For example, punctuality data may report success, while platform density, noise, heat, and rider testimony reveal stress. Likewise, a route-planning algorithm may report a faster path while accessibility logs show that the route is unusable for a rider relying on elevators, tactile paving, or low-cognitive-load wayfinding.

Ethical guardrails: multimodal transit analysis should use consent, anonymization, aggregation, data minimization, and community review. The goal is not to score or monitor individual riders. The goal is to identify where infrastructure quietly transfers cognitive, emotional, and bodily costs onto commuters who have the least power to opt out.

Revised claim: a liveable city is not the city that moves the highest number of bodies through the network with the fewest seconds of delay. It is the city that reduces unnecessary mental load across the entire travel chain: planning, walking, waiting, transferring, riding, exiting, and recovering after the trip.

Operational Questions for a Multimodal Liveability Index

Weighting: How should a city balance hard performance data, such as delay minutes, against softer but socially crucial indicators, such as perceived safety, noise, crowding, and wayfinding strain?

Equity: Which multimodal signals reveal that AI-driven efficiency is benefiting central, affluent, or already-mobile riders while pushing uncertainty and stress onto peripheral communities?

Accountability: What public reporting format would make the cognitive tax visible without exposing individual riders to surveillance or behavioral prediction?

Shared Closing

Livability Must Be Measured Through Access

A livable city is not only dense, green, or well-connected. It must also be affordable, sustainable, accessible, and socially inclusive. For decision-makers, the practical recommendation is clear: evaluate every major urban improvement by who can afford it, who benefits from it, who may be displaced by it, and whether it strengthens everyday life over time.
